All About Benzene

Compound Name & Formula

Benzene
C6H6
A hexagonal ring of 6 carbon atoms, each bonded to one hydrogen atom. Alternating double bonds

Functional Groups:
The main functional group in benzene is the aromatic ring, which gives it chemical stability.

Chemical & Physical Properties

  • Boiling point is 80.1°C
  • Melting point is 5.5°C
  • Benzene is only slightly soluble in water
  • Density is 0.8765 g/cm³
  • It has a high vapor pressure
  • Molar mass of 78.114 g·mol−1
  • Colourless and has a sweet odour

Sources of Benzene

  • Natural sources of benzene include forest fires and volcanoes
  • Car exhaust
  • Burning coal and oil
  • Cigarette smoke
  • Gasoline
  • Industrial emissions from factories that make plastics, lubricants, dyes, detergents, and pesticides
  • Household products such as glues, paints, furniture wax, and detergents

Health Effects

Short term effects

Breathing in high doses of benzene can mess with your nervous system pretty fast. People who have been exposed often report:

  • Dizziness
  • Drowsiness
  • Headaches
  • Tremors or confusion
  • Unconsciousness in extreme cases

If someone eats or drinks something contaminated with benzene it can cause:

  • Vomiting
  • Stomach irritation
  • Sleepiness
  • Convulsions
  • Rapid heartbeat

Even just touching benzene whether it’s liquid or vapor can irritate the skin, eyes, and throat. Skin contact might lead to redness and even blisters. This stuff is no joke.

Long-term exposure can:

  • Affect bone marrow
  • Cause blood disorders such as anemia or a decrease in white blood cells
  • Cause reproductive issues
  • Be linked to leukemia, especially acute myelogenous leukemia (AML)

Environmental Impact

Benzene in the air can react with other pollutants to form smog, contributing to poor air quality. It can also attach to rain or snow and contaminate soil and water. This pollution harms ecosystems fish and aquatic life exposed to benzene often get sick, stop reproducing, or die early. Plants may show stunted growth or die when the soil is contaminated. In communities near industrial sites or highways, where benzene exposure is more common, these environmental effects also impact human health, food sources, and overall quality of life.

Workplace Safety

  • Benzene is flammable and carcinogenic
  • Prolonged exposure can damage organs including liver and kidneys
  • Use proper ventilation and protective equipment such as gloves and goggles
  • Limit exposure and read labels
  • Use less harmful alternatives when possible

Disposal

Benzene is hazardous waste and cannot be thrown in the garbage or poured down the drain.

Proper disposal includes:

  • Taking contaminated materials to hazardous waste collection sites
  • Using designated waste management services for chemical waste
  • Never dumping into the environment

Improper disposal can contaminate groundwater, harm wildlife, and damage ecosystems for years.

What We Can Do as a Community

  • Reduce car usage
  • Avoid products with high levels of VOCs
  • Properly ventilate when using cleaning or painting products
  • Support environmental regulations
  • Advocate for stricter air quality laws
  • Educate others about benzene
  • Participate in clean-up initiatives
  • Monitor air quality as a community
